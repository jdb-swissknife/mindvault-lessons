Lesson 2
Teach Your Agent a New Trick
Prompting + Idea Capture -- turn your agent into a sounding board

A Quick Lesson Before We Start

In lesson 1, your subscription tracker was already set up. You just started talking.

This time is different. You're going to teach your agent a new skill yourself.

Here's the thing most people don't realize: you don't need to be technical to do this. You don't need to code anything. You just need to describe what you want clearly.

That's what prompting is. And it's the single most important skill you'll develop as an agent owner -- because once you can teach your agent one thing, you can teach it anything.

We'll walk through it step by step. By the end, you'll have a new skill running and you'll know how to create the next one on your own.

The Problem With Ideas

You have ideas all the time. In the truck between jobs. In the shower. At 11 PM when you should be sleeping.

And what happens to them?

  • You tell yourself "I'll write that down later" (you don't)
  • You scribble it on a napkin (you lose it)
  • You type it in your phone notes (you never look at it again)
  • You mention it to someone and they say "that's interesting" (nothing happens)

Six months later you think "didn't I have an idea about that?" and you can't remember the details.

Your best ideas are dying in the moment. Not because they're bad -- because you have no system to catch them.

The Solution

Your agent is now your idea capture partner. Here's how it works:

  1. Catch -- Tell your agent an idea in whatever words come out of your mouth
  2. Clarify -- It asks you 1-2 quick follow-up questions to sharpen the idea
  3. Remember -- Stores it with topics and themes so you can find it later
  4. Search -- Ask "what ideas have I had about hiring?" and get every matching idea
  5. Review -- Every Friday, see everything you captured that week

No apps. No notebooks. No "I'll remember that." Just talk to your agent.

Step 1

Teach Your Agent How to Capture Ideas

This is the prompting part. You're going to give your agent clear instructions for a new job. Read through the prompt below, then copy and paste the whole thing to your agent:

I want you to be my idea capture partner. Here's how it should work: 1. When I say "I have an idea" or "idea:" followed by something, save it. Tag it with any topics or themes you notice (marketing, hiring, operations, etc.). 2. After saving an idea, ask me 1-2 follow-up questions to help me think it through. Keep your questions short and practical. 3. When I ask "what ideas have I had about [topic]?" search your memory and give me everything related to that topic. 4. When I say "show me my ideas" give me a numbered list of all my ideas, newest first, with a one-line summary of each. 5. Every Friday, if I haven't asked already, remind me to do a quick idea review and show me what I captured that week. Save this as a skill or remember it however works best, so it sticks across all our conversations going forward.

Your agent will confirm it understands and is ready to go.

Why This Prompt Works (The Prompting Lesson)

Notice what makes this prompt effective:

ElementWhat It DoesExample
Trigger wordsTells the agent when to act"When I say 'I have an idea' or 'idea:'"
Step-by-step processTells the agent what to doSteps 1-5, each one clear
Specific formatsTells the agent how to respond"Numbered list, newest first"
PersistenceTells the agent to remember"Save this so it sticks"
The prompting formula: Every time you want your agent to do something new, use this structure:

1. When should it act?
2. What should it do?
3. How should it respond?
4. Remember it for next time

Safety rule: Never paste passwords, credit card numbers, private keys, or login codes into chat. If your agent needs access to something, ask it to walk you through a safe setup instead.

That's it. That's prompting. You just learned it.
Step 2

Try It Out

Here are some things to say to your agent to test it:

Capture an idea (full thought):

"I have an idea -- what if I started a referral program where existing customers get a discount for sending new customers our way?"

Capture an idea (quick and dirty):

"idea: get the truck wrapped with our logo"

Capture a half-baked thought:

"Random thought -- what if we offered weekend emergency service at a premium rate?"

Your agent saves every one and asks a couple questions to help you think it through.

Search your ideas:

"What ideas have I had about marketing?"

See everything:

"Show me all my ideas"

Brainstorm on one:

"Let's flesh out my idea about the referral program"

Weekly review:

"Review my ideas from this week"

The difference here is the follow-up questions. Your agent doesn't just file the idea away -- it pushes back. Asks things like:

  • "Have you thought about how you'd track the referrals?"
  • "What would the discount be -- percentage or flat amount?"
  • "Is this something for this quarter or more of a next-year idea?"

This is what makes it a thinking partner, not just a notepad.

Step 3

Building the Habit

The tracker works because you set it up once and it runs itself. Idea capture is different -- you have to build the habit of talking to your agent.

Week 1: The Dump

  • Sit down for 10 minutes and brain-dump every idea you've been sitting on
  • Doesn't matter if it's good or bad -- just get it out of your head
  • Your agent will sort and tag them

Week 2+: The Daily

  • Once a day, tell your agent 1-2 things that crossed your mind
  • Morning with coffee, end of the day, whenever
  • It takes 30 seconds. The agent does the heavy lifting

Friday: The Review

  • Ask your agent to show you the week's ideas
  • Star the ones worth pursuing
  • Let go of the ones that don't hold up

After a couple weeks you'll stop thinking "I should write that down" and start thinking "I should tell my agent."

Why This Matters Beyond Ideas

The idea capture teaches you something important: your agent can be proactive.

The subscription tracker was reactive -- you give it data, it watches. The idea capture goes further. Your agent:

  • Asks you questions (not just answers them)
  • Connects themes across multiple ideas
  • Remembers context from conversations you forgot you had

Now imagine applying the same pattern to:

Step 4

Your First Assignment

Your to-do list:
1. Copy the prompt from Step 1 and paste it to your agent
2. Confirm it understood -- ask "do you know how to capture my ideas?"
3. Give it 3-4 ideas right now (half-baked is fine)
4. Ask "show me all my ideas" to see what it captured
5. Try searching: "what ideas have I had about [topic]?"

Once you see your ideas organized and tagged, you'll get it.

The Bigger Picture

Lesson 1 taught you that your agent remembers and reminds.

Lesson 2 taught you two things:

  • Your agent thinks with you (idea capture)
  • You can teach your agent new skills by describing what you want (prompting)
The real takeaway: You don't have to wait for someone to build something. You can create your own tools whenever you want:

"When I say X, do Y. Respond like Z. Remember this."

That's the formula. Use it for anything.

Here's what's coming next:

  • Your agent can find information for you -- research competitors, pricing, best practices
  • Your agent can connect your tools -- email, calendar, documents
  • Your agent can run your business systems -- lead tracking, follow-ups, reporting

Each lesson adds a new capability. But they all follow the same pattern you've already learned:

You describe the problem. The agent handles it.
